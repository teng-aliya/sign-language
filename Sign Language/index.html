who speaks sign language?

the deaf community

A person who has a hearing impairment to any degree is to be considered either deaf or hard of hearing. Sign language is considered one of the sole ways to communicate with the deaf and hard of hearing community.

the mute community

A person who has an inability to speak, whether it's due to congenital deafness, brain damage, or psychological causes, is to be considered mute. In most cases, sign language might be the only form of communication with the mute community, asides from writing.

Currently, there are approximately 40,000 people who are legally part of the deaf community in Malaysia. For those who are not registered as legally deaf aren't counted under the census possibly due to multiple registrations issues. This number may also consider those who are part of the mute community as deaf, thus the exact number of those part of the deaf community and the mute community are unknown.

History

  • Penang Sign Language predates the official sign language due to deaf students who attended to Federation School for the Deaf (FSD) who learnt oral skills, not sign language. As a result, the students would sign by themselves in the dormitory of FSD every night. FSD was established by Lady Templer, the wife of the British High Commissioner in Malaya.

  • In the early 1960s in Johor, Mr. Tan Yap, a pharmacist, took an unpaid leave several times to take courses in interpretation and sign language in America. He brought back his knowledge of ASL or American Sign Language with him. Mr Tan started teaching deaf children, nurses and some other hearing people how to sign. However, Tan Yap's suggestions were rejected by the Government.

  • An American, Professor Frances Parsons, travelled around the world in order to introduce Total Communication and Sign Language to poor schools for the deaf, in order to better prepare them for education. In the same year, Frances Parsons went to Kuala Lumpur to meet with Dr Mahathir bin Mohamad, the Minister of Education. After a 45-minute discussion, Dr Mahathir agreed with Parsons's suggestions and theory. In the next few days, Dr. Mahathir announced new legislation that obliged schools in Malaysia to teach Total Communication and Sign Language. Manchester-trained teachers were unhappy with the new law and protested Mohamad's decision.

  • Malaysian Sign Language was created with the establishment of the Malaysian Federation of the Deaf in 1998, and its use has expanded among deaf leaders and participants. It is based on American Sign Language (ASL), but the two are considered different languages.

  • The Malaysian Government recognises Bahasa Isyarat Malaysia as an Official Language that can be used to communicate with the deaf and mute community. The language began to be used as a form of communication with the community during official announcements and broadcasting.

Languages and dialects

BAHASA ISYARAT MALAYSIA

THE OFFICIAL MALAYSIAN SIGN LANGUAGE

the official sign language used by the Malaysian government to communicate with the deaf community and was officially recognised by the Malaysian government in 2008.

AMERICAN SIGN LANGUAGE

THE ENGLISH EQUIVALENT

The english sign language that was brought from America by Mr Tan Yap in 1960s and introduced to the Prime Minister by Professor Frances Parsons in 1976. It served as the basis of Bahasa Isyarat Malaysia.

SELANGOR/KUALA LUMPUR SIGN LANGUAGE

DIALECT

A dialect that was based off the American Sign Language, which had diverged significantly enough to now be considered a language in its own right.

PULAU PENANG SIGN LANGUAGE

DIALECT

A dialect developed by deaf students of Federation School for the Deaf back in 1954 when students were taught oral skills, not sign language.

KOD TANGAN BAHASA MALAYSIA

MANUALLY CODED MALAY

A method to teach Malay to deaf students in formal education settings. It is not a language, by any means.
